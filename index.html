Welcome to SUN LAB

At SUN Lab, we harness the power of focused ultrasound (FUS), neuroimaging, and immunoengineering to revolutionize the treatment of neurological disorders by opening the blood-brain barrier. Our mission is to unveil the mechanisms through which FUS modulates the neuroimmune disease/aging environment, developing innovative ultrasound-based techniques for treating and monitoring conditions like glioblastoma and Alzheimer’s disease, setting a new course for the future of neuro-immune interface research.

Focused ultrasound mediated drug delivery

Focused ultrasound mediated drug delivery is an emerging technique that combines the use of ultrasound waves with nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems. This innovative technique offers several advantages over traditional drug delivery methods, including increased targeting precision, reduced systemic side effects, and improved therapeutic efficacy. At Sun Lab, our aim is to improve drug delivery by developing a specialized nanoparticle formulation. Our formulation encapsulates a hydrophobic drug alongside an ultrasound-sensitive contrast agent. Our targeting strategy involves polymer conjugation with DBCO-Azide click chemistry molecules, ensuring precise delivery to the target site. When exposed to an ultrasonic field, these polymeric nanoparticles demonstrate stable cavitation, enabling controlled drug release precisely at the intended location.

Further advancements in this research focus on enhancing the efficiency of drug penetration and distribution within brain tissue, aiming to address the significant challenges posed by the blood-brain barrier in treating neurological conditions. By leveraging focused ultrasound technology, we envision creating a new paradigm for non-invasive, targeted therapy applications in neurology.

Focused Ultrasound Mediated Neuro-Immunomodulation

The use of focused ultrasound in conjunction with microbubbles represents a cutting-edge delivery modality capable of efficiently transporting immune cells and therapeutic agents across the blood-brain barrier (BBB). The BBB, a selectively permeable membrane, plays a critical role in controlling molecule inflow and outflow into the brain, thus presenting considerable challenges in delivering systemic therapeutics to the brain. At Sun Lab, we leverage focused ultrasound and microbubbles to transiently and reversibly open the BBB, enhancing its permeability without permanently altering its structure. This localized, non-invasive approach permits the entry of immune cells and therapeutics, as well as the exit of potential antigenic markers across the BBB, paving the way for improved management of neurological diseases.

This advanced methodology promotes a novel paradigm in neuro-immunomodulation, aiming to mitigate the effects of various neurological disorders by facilitating the controlled delivery of therapeutic agents. By precisely modulating the neuroimmune environment, we aspire to unveil new horizons in the treatment of diseases that have remained elusive due to the restrictive nature of the BBB.

Cavitation Imaging and Control

The imaging group's cavitation imaging project focuses on employing advanced computational techniques, including machine learning and parallelism, to enhance the field of passive cavitation imaging. The primary objective is to refine the in-clinic capability for real-time beamforming of cavitation maps, thereby elevating the effectiveness of therapeutic interventions.

This initiative also explores the integration of closed-loop systems for monitoring cavitation dosage autonomously, eliminating the need for manual interpretation. An exemplar system is designed to regulate acoustic pressures to favor stable cavitation over inertial cavitation, which can lead to undesired bubble expansion and elevated tissue temperatures. Additionally, we investigate the utilization of dual transducers operating at slight frequency offsets to produce a beat pattern, mimicking a pulse repetition frequency (PRF) in a continuous wave format, aimed at optimizing therapeutic outcomes.
